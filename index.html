Part 3: Wheat and Tares

Read Matthew 13:24–30. Once you understand there are two races (in our temple and on the earth), you can begin to grasp why we are a kingdom divided. And why the ruling class hate us and lie to us. We may all look the same on the outside, but inside there is something different. Jesus said you will know them by their fruit — do they lie? Do they honor the Father and the Son?

Jesus explained that the wheat are the children of the kingdom, sown by the Son of Man, and the tares are the children of the wicked one, sown by the enemy. This powerful parable reveals not only the battle between good and evil but also a hidden mystery: that we, the wheat, are not merely human, but spiritual beings planted by God — angels in host bodies, foreknown before the foundation of the world.

This division of seedlines originates in Genesis. God told Adam in Genesis 2:17 that eating from the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil would bring death. Then in Genesis 3:15, God declares enmity between the serpent's seed and the woman’s seed. That conflict carries on throughout the biblical narrative — even to Revelation 12:17 where the dragon makes war with the remnant of her seed, and Revelation 21:17 where measurement is made according to the measure of a man, that is, of the angel. These are not metaphorical ideas — they speak to spiritual genetics, two opposing lineages.

This explains why we feel like strangers in the world. We are not of it — we are from above. The enemy has worked tirelessly to blend the two races into one indistinguishable body, but the spiritual fruit reveals the truth.

Part 4: Twin System — Opposing Energies

We are born into a snare with two opposing spirits — a twin system. This is the mystery revealed throughout Scripture. From the beginning, there has been enmity between two seedlines, a war within the temple, and opposing forces within man: light and darkness, male and female, good and evil. These energies reflect the inner war that Paul speaks of in Romans 7 — the flesh striving against the spirit.

Cain and Abel were twins born of two different fathers: one of God, the other of the serpent. Cain represents the carnal, earthly energy — the one who brought death into the field. Abel represents righteousness — the spiritual man, the image of Christ. The entire world system follows the Cain model: competition, envy, dominance, pride. The Kingdom of God follows the Abel pattern: humility, meekness, obedience, and sacrifice.

This is echoed in ancient symbols like the Yin and Yang — not as new-age ideas, but as representations of the dual nature in the flesh. Male and female, light and dark — not physical gender, but spiritual coding in a fallen system. Only in Christ are these two opposing energies reconciled. He makes the two one. He breaks down the middle wall of partition.

As long as we remain double-minded and unconverted, we are in a constant war within ourselves. But through the Holy Spirit, Christ removes the corrupt seed and replaces it with the incorruptible. That is the mystery of the new birth. That is what brings peace — the good you and the bad you are reconciled into one new creature.

Part 5: Genesis & Elohim

In Genesis 1 the Lord God Almighty is not mentioned. The Hebrew word for God (H430) is 'Elohim' — plural, meaning gods, angels, and magistrates of the Supreme God. The Lord God is only mentioned in Genesis 2 when He forms Adam, the first man with a living soul.

Genesis 2:2 says "God (Elohim) ended His work (deputyship) and rested." Ask yourself, would the Lord God Almighty be the Deputy? No, He'd be the Sheriff. Would He need rest? No, but Elohim, now in flesh, might need a rest.

These verses suggest a deeper truth hidden in plain sight — that many beings called "gods" (Elohim) were involved in shaping this world, under the allowance of the Most High. This implies that the world system was built not by the direct hand of El — the singular, supreme God — but by spiritual beings operating under His authority, some of whom later rebelled.

The soul that God breathes into Adam — and into us — originates not from below but from above. We are not just creations; we are offspring. Angels placed into host bodies. Jesus said, "Ye are gods" (Psalm 82, John 10) and Paul affirms we were chosen in Christ before the foundation of the world (Ephesians 1:4). That means God foreknew us — we are not accidents of dust, but divine intentions planted in flesh.

This is why conversion is a literal turning — a spiritual inversion. The spirit from above reclaims what was taken below. That which was sown into darkness must be brought into light.

Tit 3:2 To speak evil of no man, to be no brawlers, but gentle, shewing all meekness unto all men.

Tit 3:3 For we ourselves also were sometimes foolish, disobedient, deceived, serving divers lusts and pleasures, living in malice and envy, hateful, and hating one another.

Tit 3:4 But after that the kindness and love of God our Saviour toward man appeared,

Tit 3:5 Not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to his mercy he saved us, by the washing of regeneration, and renewing of the Holy Ghost;
